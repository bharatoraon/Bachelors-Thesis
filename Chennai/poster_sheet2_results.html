Scenario C — Combined Integration: Timetable Synchronisation + Physical FOB Infrastructure

The combined scenario achieves the greatest overall TFI reduction. Critically, the analysis demonstrates that operational interventions (schedule sync) yield far higher structural friction reduction than physical walkway investments alone. Infrastructure alone cannot overcome 45–90-minute headways at peripheral suburban stations. Timetable integration is the primary lever — FOBs amplify but cannot substitute.

Policy Priority: Operational schedule integration first — then targeted FOB construction at high-friction nodes.
08Four Core Conclusions
C1

Transfer friction is not evenly distributed — it is a spatially inequitable burden concentrated in suburban rail nodes at the city periphery.

C2

Peak-hour penalties (up to 90+ min) at VOC Nagar and Veppampattu represent a temporal inequity trap for outer-city commuters.

C3

Timetable synchronisation is the most impactful single policy lever — alone reducing average TFI by ~40% without physical construction.

C4

Gate-to-gate polygon analysis proves centroid methods underestimate walk friction, invalidating prior spatial accessibility analyses at interchange level.

09Policy Recommendations
P1

Unified Timetable Integration Authority

Statutory inter-agency body (CMRL–TNSTC–Southern Railway) mandating coordinated departure schedules. Maximum 15-min cross-modal wait at all interchange nodes.

P2

Peripheral Suburban Rail Frequency

Increase off-peak Southern Railway headways from 45–90 min to ≤20 min on high-TFI corridors — targeting VOC Nagar, Veppampattu, Pattaravakkam.

P3

Targeted FOB Construction

Prioritise physical walkway improvements at nodes where walk distance exceeds 250 m — specifically high-TFI bus-rail transfer points in southern and northern corridors.

P4

Real-Time TFI Monitoring

Adopt TFI as a statutory transit equity metric in Chennai's MaaS platform — enabling continuous temporal inequity monitoring and data-driven schedule revision.
