The Transfer Friction Index — Final Formula
TFI = Wₜ + Ww + ( Pmiss × Ww ) — output in MINUTES per transfer
Wₜ
Gate-to-Gate Walk Time
Physical walk duration from exit gate of Mode A to entry gate of Mode B. Computed as: Distance (m) ÷ 1.2 m/s (HCM standard pedestrian speed).

Measured from precise GeoJSON polygon gates — not centroids.
Ww
Expected Wait Time — Headway Proxy
Average inter-departure headway at the receiving stop for the connecting mode. Computed from GTFS stop_times.txt as chronological differences between successive departures.

Filtered: 0 < headway < 3 hours. Default = 15 min where data sparse.
Pmiss × Ww
Missed Connection Penalty
Expected additional time cost if the connecting vehicle is missed due to the walking time. Pmiss scales with walk duration and service headway.

Pmiss = clip[(Wt/600)×(Ww/1800), 0.05, 0.40]

Starting from first principles: what are all the time costs unique to a transfer event — distinct from in-vehicle time?

Wardman (2004), Guo & Wilson (2011) → Walk + Wait + Penalty. Generalised cost framework. Identified the three-part structure.

GTFS → walk distance + headways. Gate GeoJSON → accurate walk distance. No real-time data → must use probabilistic reliability proxy.

Bates et al. (2001), Liu & Sinha (2007) → miss probability increases with walk time AND headway. Formulated as a product, clipped to realistic range [0.05–0.40].

Each term is orthogonal. Output is in minutes — a universal, comparable unit across modes, zones, and time periods. Completeness argument confirmed.

Gives me: The foundational additive Generalised Cost structure (in-vehicle + walk + wait + penalty). My TFI applies this decomposition exclusively to the transfer portion of a trip — with all components in consistent time units.

Bates, J., Polak, J., Jones, P. & Cook, A. (2001)
"The Valuation of Reliability for Personal Travel"
Transportation Research Part E, 37(2–3), 191–229
Gives me: The foundational concept: schedule reliability has a quantifiable time-equivalent monetary value to passengers. My Pmiss×Ww term is a computational translation of this — converting reliability risk directly into equivalent missed minutes.

Liu, R. & Sinha, S. (2007)
"Modelling Urban Bus Service and Passenger Reliability"
TRB Annual Meeting Paper 07–0414
Gives me: The structural relationship that miss probability is a joint function of walk duration (longer walk = more time consuming = higher miss risk) AND headway (lower frequency = higher cost of missing). My formula `(Wt/600) × (Ww/1800)` models exactly this product relationship.

Hörcher, D., Graham, D.J. & Anderson, R.J. (2017)
"Crowding Cost Estimation with Large Scale Smart Card Data"
Transportation Research Part B, 95, 105–123
Gives me: Precedent for incorporating probabilistic penalty terms (risk-adjusted, frequency-scaled) into passenger generalised cost functions. Validates the approach of adding an expected-value penalty term to a deterministic base cost.

🟢 Metro → Metro transfer (low friction)

Walk: 2 min (120s) · Headway: 6 min (360s)

Pmiss = clip[(120/600) × (360/1800), 0.05, 0.40]

= clip[0.20 × 0.20, 0.05, 0.40] = clip[0.04, ...]

→ floored to 0.05 → Penalty = 0.05 × 6 = 0.3 min

Metro is so frequent that even if you miss it, you wait only 6 more minutes × 5% = negligible.

🔴 Bus → Suburban Rail transfer (high friction)

Walk: 6 min (360s) · Headway: 60 min (3600s)

Pmiss = clip[(360/600) × (3600/1800), 0.05, 0.40]

= clip[0.60 × 2.0, 0.05, 0.40] = clip[1.2, ...]

→ capped at 0.40 → Penalty = 0.40 × 60 = 24 min

At peripheral suburban nodes, the probability of missing a train is high and the cost of missing it is enormous.

The Completeness Argument — Nothing Missing, No Double-Counting
🚶Stage 1

Physical Movement Cost

The passenger exits Mode A and physically walks to Mode B's boarding point. Every second of this walk is unproductive transfer-specific time. Fully deterministic — computable from distance and speed.

WₜDeterministic
⏳Stage 2

Idle Waiting Cost

Once at the boarding point, the passenger waits. This is the most psychologically burdensome component (2–3× in-vehicle time value, Wardman 2004). Probabilistic average — derived from headway as proxy.

WwProbabilistic avg.
🎲Stage 3

Reliability Failure Cost

If the walk took too long and the vehicle left, the passenger must wait an additional full headway. This expected penalty grows with both walk duration and headway length. Expected value of a Bernoulli failure event.

Pmiss×WwExpected value
Completeness: A transfer involves exactly these three time costs and no others. They are mutually exclusive (Wₜ happens before boarding; Ww happens at platform; Pmiss×Ww is an expected penalty — not actual extra wait). The formula is complete and non-redundant.

Viva Defence — 4 Hard Questions & Answers
Q1"Why not just use walk + wait? Why do you need a third term?"
AWalk + wait captures the average experienced cost. But at peripheral suburban nodes with 60-min headways, a 6-minute walk means a ~40% chance of missing the train — incurring another 60 minutes. Ignoring this dramatically underestimates the structural burden. The Pmiss term converts reliability risk into equivalent time, making the index sensitive to exactly where inequity is highest: low-frequency peripheral nodes.
Q2"Why not use survey-calibrated disutility weights like 2.5× for waiting?"
ADisutility weights are context-, mode-, and culture-specific — calibrating them requires extensive passenger stated preference surveys, which are beyond the GTFS-only scope of this study. My TFI deliberately uses time-unit consistent components to enable objective, reproducible comparison across 8,000+ edges and three modes without subjective calibration. Transferability to other Indian cities is preserved.
Q3"The Pmiss formula looks like a heuristic — can you validate it?"
ACorrect — it is a structured heuristic grounded in two literature-validated structural relationships (Liu & Sinha, 2007): miss probability increases with walk duration AND headway length. The clip bounds [0.05, 0.40] prevent unrealistic extremes. Its purpose is to rank interchange nodes by friction burden, not to produce exact probabilities. For exact calibration, real-time arrival data and a stochastic simulation would be needed — a clearly stated limitation of this study.
Q4"How is TFI different from existing GTFS accessibility metrics?"
AGTFS accessibility scores (r5r, peartree, OpenTripPlanner) answer: "where can you reach in 30 minutes?" — a demand-side reachability question. TFI answers: "how much hidden time penalty exists at each structural transfer node?" — a supply-side interchange performance question. TFI is an interchange-level diagnostic metric, not an origin-level accessibility score. The two are complementary, not competing.
