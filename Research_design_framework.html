The Research Design Wheel

Research Phase Architecture

  • P1
    Problem Framing & ConceptualisationIdentify temporal gaps in transit planning; premise TFI as measurable construct; situate within Indian urban context — Chennai as case city.
  • P2
    Data Acquisition & StandardisationGTFS ingestion (CMRL, MTC, SR); filter 1.7M stop-times to Weekday services; parse calendar.txt; load gate polygon GeoJSON.
  • P3
    Network ConstructionBuild time-expanded directed graph; snap geometries to gate polygons; compute ~8,000 walk transfer edges using gate-to-gate impedance ≤ 500 m.
  • P4
    TFI Computation & Temporal SegmentationCalculate per-node headways; apply TFI formula segmented by Peak (08–10, 17–19) and Off-Peak windows.
  • P5
    Equity Analysis & Ward AggregationCompute Gini Coefficient of TFI; link ward-level demographics; identify spatial friction hotspots via GIS.
  • P6
    Policy Simulation & RecommendationsSimulate Scenario A (Timetable Sync), B (FOB Infra), C (Combined); derive actionable planning proposals.

Central Research Questions
  • RQ1
    What is the magnitude of transfer friction?To what extent do intermodal transfer waiting times, walking distances, and missed connection probabilities generate measurable passenger time penalties at interchange nodes in Chennai's multimodal network?
  • RQ2
    How is transfer friction spatially distributed?Does the distribution of TFI across Chennai's interchange nodes reflect spatial inequity — i.e., do peripheral zones systematically bear higher transfer burdens than central core areas?
  • RQ3
    How does temporal variability shape the burden?Do peak-hour conditions versus off-peak conditions create significantly different TFI profiles? Are certain nodes disproportionately worse at specific times of day?
  • RQ4
    How do conventional accessibility metrics compare?In what ways does traditional proximity-based spatial accessibility overestimate effective access when temporal reliability is incorporated? What is the magnitude of this overestimation?
  • RQ5
    What policy interventions are most effective?Between operational interventions (timetable synchronisation) and physical infrastructure improvements (FOBs/subways), which yields greater reduction in transfer friction — and for which population groups?

Epistemological & Philosophical Position
🔬
Post-Positivist Ontology
Reality of temporal inequity in transit is measurable and exists independently of observer. GTFS schedule data provides objective evidence of structural friction.
📊
Quantitative Epistemology
Knowledge is produced through systematic measurement, deductive reasoning, and statistical analysis. The TFI metric operationalises abstract inequity into a scalar value.
🗺
Applied / Policy-Oriented
Research outputs are designed for direct applicability in urban transport planning practice and governance frameworks in Indian metropolitan contexts.

Theoretical Framework — Four Pillars
⏳
Time Geography
(Hägerstrand, 1970)
Travel behaviour as trajectories in space-time. Transfer friction modelled as a temporal constraint — time paths disrupted by schedule misalignment at interchange nodes.
🌐
Network Theory
(Watts & Strogatz)
Multimodal transit as a directed graph. Betweenness centrality identifies critical nodes. Time-expanded network captures schedule feasibility as graph topology.
⚖
Transport Equity
(Pereira et al., 2017)
Accessibility as a social good. Gini-based distribution of TFI operationalises welfare economics to assess distributional fairness across population groups.
📡
GTFS & Multimodal
Schedule Analysis
General Transit Feed Specification as standardised data layer. Time-expanded networks derived from GTFS enable computational modelling of schedule-dependent accessibility.
⬡
Variable Architecture
Dependent Variable
Transfer Friction Index (TFI)
Composite scalar (minutes) integrating walk time + wait time + missed connection penalty. Computed per interchange node, segmented by peak/off-peak.
Derived Outcome
Time-Based Accessibility Score
Proportion of destinations reachable within 30/45/60-min thresholds. Aggregated at ward level and compared against proximity-based measures.
Independent Variable 1
Schedule Headway (Wait Time)
Average inter-departure interval at each stop for receiving mode. Key driver of TFI — low frequency → high TFI. Computed from GTFS stop_times.txt.
Independent Variable 2
Gate-to-Gate Walk Distance
Physical walk impedance between entry/exit polygon centroids. Computed from precise GeoJSON gates — corrects centroid-bias of prior studies.
Control Variable
Transit Mode Type
CMRL Metro / MTC Bus / Southern Railway suburban rail. Modal attributes (frequency, stop density, coverage) influence baseline TFI profile.
Moderating Variable
Spatial Zone (Core vs Peripheral)
Zone classification (Central Core, Inner Ring, North/South Peripheral). Moderates relationship between mode type and TFI level — peripheral amplifies burden.
