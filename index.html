Transfer Friction & Temporal Inequity in Chennai’s Multimodal Public Transport Network

Bharat Oraon  ·  Roll No. 2220200359  ·  Urban & Regional Planning  ·  B.Plan Final Year

Chennai’s public transport network — comprising CMRL Metro, MTC Bus fleet, and Southern Railway suburban network — serves over 5.85 million residents across the Greater Chennai Corporation (GCC). Despite significant capital investment in new Metro corridors, the quality of intermodal transfers between these three systems remains unmeasured, unplanned, and inequitably distributed.

This thesis develops a Transfer Friction Index (TFI) from raw GTFS schedule data for 642 intermodal transfer hubs across GCC, disaggregating friction into peak and off-peak temporal periods. Spatial analysis reveals a Gini coefficient of 0.185 — moderate but structurally significant inequity concentrated in peripheral suburban-dependent wards. Seven hubs record TFI exceeding 60 minutes, all on the suburban rail network. Demand-weighted analysis using the ChennaiOne mobility API (7.87 million validated trips) quantifies total daily friction burden at 18,389 passenger-hours. Policy simulation demonstrates that timetable synchronisation outperforms physical infrastructure improvements in equity terms, while their combination achieves a 23% reduction in mean TFI and a 19% improvement in the Gini coefficient.

Chapter 01

Introduction

The structural blind spot in Chennai’s transit planning — and why measuring transfer quality changes what we see.

1.1 Research Problem & Motivation

Chennai’s public transport planning has, for three decades, been dominated by a singular spatial logic: extend the network, add new stations, commission new corridors. The Chennai Metro Rail Limited (CMRL), inaugurated in 2015, has expanded the city’s rail transit footprint significantly, and ambitious Phase 2 and Phase 3 proposals promise further corridors. Yet this expansion-first logic contains a structural blind spot.

A passenger who rides a CMRL Metro train to its terminus at St. Thomas Mount, and then waits forty-five minutes on a poorly-lit platform for the next Southern Railway suburban train to continue their journey, has not been meaningfully served by the infrastructure investment that brought them that far. The Metro line delivered them to the edge of a service gap — and that gap is invisible in the maps and ridership statistics that planners use to evaluate success.

This thesis makes that gap visible. It develops a Transfer Friction Index (TFI) — a computationally reproducible metric derived from raw GTFS schedule data — and applies it to every one of the 642 intermodal transfer hubs in the Greater Chennai Corporation. The results reveal a structurally inequitable distribution of transfer burden, concentrated in peripheral, transit-dependent, lower-income wards, and worsening significantly during off-peak hours when the city’s most economically vulnerable commuters travel.

Core Argument Chennai’s transit challenge is not primarily one of missing infrastructure — it is one of missing coordination. Three independent operators share the same city, the same passengers, and zero common scheduling authority. The Transfer Friction Index measures the cost of that coordination failure, minute by minute, hub by hub.

The study is bounded by the GCC administrative boundary (426 sq.km) — a deliberate choice that maximises GTFS data completeness. The broader Chennai Metropolitan Area (CMA, 1,189 sq.km) was excluded because GTFS coverage falls below 40% beyond the GCC boundary, making TFI computation unreliable for outer zones. Temporally, only weekday GTFS schedules are analysed to eliminate weekend service variability and focus on the commuter transit burden.

1.3 Research Objectives

  1. To quantify intermodal transfer friction at all 642 transfer hubs in GCC using a composite Transfer Friction Index (TFI) derived from GTFS schedules.
  2. To disaggregate TFI by peak and off-peak temporal periods and identify hubs exhibiting significant temporal deterioration.
  3. To spatially analyse TFI distribution across GCC’s 200 wards and measure distributional equity using the Gini coefficient.
  4. To validate TFI scores against real demand data from the ChennaiOne mobility platform API (7.87 million trips).
  5. To simulate policy interventions (timetable synchronisation, infrastructure improvement, combined) and assess differential impacts on TFI reduction and equity.

1.4 Significance & Contributions

First GTFS-derived TFI for an Indian Metro

No prior study has computed a composite transfer friction metric covering all intermodal hubs in an Indian metropolitan network from raw schedule data.

Temporal Disaggregation

TFI is split into peak (08:00–10:00, 17:00–19:00) and off-peak periods — revealing a 32% average worsening in off-peak service that spatial analysis alone cannot detect.

Demand-Weighted Equity Analysis

Integration of real API ridership data (ChennaiOne, 7.87M trips) with computed friction scores produces demand-weighted burden maps that reveal where the pain is concentrated.

Replicable Open-Source Pipeline

The full Python analytical pipeline — GTFS ingestion, spatial network construction, TFI computation, hotspot analysis — is documented and applicable to any city with GTFS feeds.

Chapter 02

Literature Review

Transfer penalty theory, GTFS-based network modelling, transit equity frameworks, and the research gap this thesis addresses.

2.1 Transfer Quality & Passenger Experience

The academic literature on transfer quality has established that passengers experience transfers as disproportionately costly relative to in-vehicle travel time. Bates et al. (2001) found that transfer penalties are valued at approximately 2× the equivalent in-vehicle time — a passenger waiting 10 minutes at a transfer point experiences this as equivalent to 20 minutes of travel. Guo & Wilson (2011) further showed that psychological perception of wait time is 1.5–2× longer than actual clock time at low-frequency stops, compounding the burden. Iseki & Taylor (2009) proposed a multi-dimensional framework for transfer quality encompassing physical accessibility, service frequency, information provision, and safety — most of which remain unmeasured in Indian transit planning contexts.

The concept of “hidden wait time” is central to this thesis. When planners report average journey times or route coverage, they invisibilise the waiting time at transfer points — which this thesis shows can be as high as 108 minutes at the worst hub.

2.2 GTFS-Based Multimodal Network Modelling

The General Transit Feed Specification (GTFS) has emerged as the global standard for machine-readable transit schedule data. Wessel & Farber (2019) demonstrated that GTFS-derived accessibility measures can capture transit equity patterns with high spatial resolution. El-Geneidy et al. have used headway analysis from GTFS data to measure service variability. However, the overwhelming majority of GTFS-based studies focus on travel time or spatial accessibility — not on the quality of inter-operator transfers specifically. This study fills that gap.

2.3 Transit Equity Frameworks

Litman (2002) established a typology distinguishing horizontal equity (equal treatment of individuals) from vertical equity (differential benefits to disadvantaged groups). This thesis addresses vertical equity: the distribution of transfer burden across socioeconomic groups, measured spatially at ward level. Delbosc & Currie (2011) documented the equity-efficiency trade-off in transit planning — investments that improve mean performance often worsen distributional outcomes. This is precisely what Scenario B (infrastructure-only) reveals in this thesis.

2.4 Gini Coefficient in Transport Analysis

The Gini coefficient, conventionally used to measure income inequality, has been adapted for transit equity analysis by Jang et al. (2017) and Welch & Mishra (2013). A Gini of 0 indicates perfectly equal distribution of transit service quality; a value of 1 indicates total concentration. Chennai’s peak-period TFI Gini of 0.185 is comparable to mid-tier global cities — meaningful but actionable. The critical insight is spatial: the inequality is geographically clustered in peripheral suburban corridors, not randomly distributed.

2.5 Research Gap & Positioning

Chapter 03

Study Area

Three operators, one city, zero coordination — Chennai’s tripartite transit system and the governance gap at its heart.

3.1 Chennai’s Urban Growth & Transport Context

Founded in 1639 around Fort St. George, Chennai has grown from a colonial port settlement into India’s fourth-largest metropolitan area. Population growth — 3.84M (1991) → 4.68M (2001) → 5.85M (2011) → est. 7.26M (2025) — has outpaced transit investment in the periphery. The 2011 GCC boundary expansion from 172 sq.km to 426 sq.km incorporated 42 previously peri-urban municipalities, adding a ring of lower-density, car-scarce residential areas highly dependent on public transport.

Chennai’s transit network has a distinctive morphology: a north-south suburban rail spine (Southern Railway, built 1931–1987) complemented by a newer east-west metro axis (CMRL, 2015–present). This creates an asymmetric network — good connectivity along major corridors, poor connectivity at junctions and cross-city transfers. The MTC bus network fills the gaps but operates under entirely separate scheduling authority.

This institutional fragmentation is the proximate cause of transfer friction — a problem that cannot be solved by infrastructure alone.

3.3 Ward Structure & Demographic Context

Map: Population Density & TFI Overlay

GCC’s 200 administrative wards average 29,250 residents each, with significant variation: inner-city wards like Triplicane reach 80,000+ residents/km² while peripheral wards like Sholinganallur are under 5,000/km². Ward-level census data reveals a consistent pattern: peripheral wards with higher slum population shares, lower literacy rates, and lower car ownership also exhibit the highest TFI values. The correlation between slum population share and TFI zone membership is r = 0.62 — strong enough to confirm that transfer friction is a socioeconomic equity issue, not merely a spatial one.

3.4 GCC vs CMA Scoping Decision

The Chennai Metropolitan Area (CMA) covers 1,189 sq.km — nearly 3× the GCC area. It was excluded from this study for a specific analytical reason: GTFS coverage drops below 40% for Southern Railway stations beyond the GCC boundary (Chengalpattu–Arakkonam corridor). Including CMA would introduce systematic underestimation of TFI in outer zones due to missing service records — making equity comparisons misleading. GCC provides near-complete trimodal GTFS coverage for rigorous, comparable computation across all 200 wards.

Methodology

A seven-stage computational pipeline from raw GTFS data to demand-weighted equity analysis.

4.1 Research Design & Pipeline

Map: Spatial Transfer Network Construction (Edges)

The methodology is structured as a reproducible computational pipeline. Each stage produces a documented output that feeds the next. The pipeline is implemented in Python using pandas, GeoPandas, NetworkX, and the Shapely geometry library. All GTFS data is filtered to weekday service only.

1

Data Ingestion & Standardisation

CMRL + MTC + Southern Railway GTFS feeds loaded. Calendar.txt filtered to weekday-only services. Stops unified to single coordinate system (EPSG:4326).

→ unified_stops.geojson (10,247 stops) · unified_stop_times.csv (1,700,442 records) · unified_routes.csv
2

Spatial Transfer Network Construction

For each stop, all stops of different modes within 500m identified (Haversine). Walk distance calculated using precise metro/suburban gate-to-gate polygon centroids — not station centroids. Walk time = distance ÷ 1.2 m/s.

→ intermodal_transfers.csv (16,126 directed transfer edges) · transfer_edges_sample.gpkg
3

Graph Analysis — Betweenness Centrality

Directed topological graph built using NetworkX (10,247 nodes, 16,126+ edges). Betweenness centrality computed to identify backbone interchange hubs whose failure would cascade network-wide.

→ node_centrality.csv · Top hubs: Govt. Estate/Omandurar, Guindy, Mambalam
4

TFI Computation (Peak & Off-Peak)

Transfer Friction Index calculated for each of 642 hubs. Temporal split: Peak = 08:00–10:00 & 17:00–19:00; Off-Peak = all other hours. Average headway computed per stop from sorted departure sequences.

→ hub_friction_index_peak.csv · hub_friction_index_offpeak.csv · hub_friction_index_with_coords.csv
5

Spatial Hotspot Analysis

Gi* (Getis-Ord) spatial statistics computed at ward level. Ward TFI = mean of all hub TFIs within ward boundary. Spatial autocorrelation (Moran’s I) confirms peripheral clustering is non-random.

→ spatial_friction_hotspots.geojson · ward_tfi_for_qgis.csv · wards_equity_burden.gpkg
6

Policy Simulation (Scenarios A / B / C)

Scenario A: Cap wait time at 15 min (timetable synchronisation). Scenario B: Reduce walk distance by 30% (FOB/walkway infrastructure). Scenario C: Both combined. Gini coefficient recomputed for each scenario.

→ Policy comparison table · Equity trade-off analysis · Three-phase roadmap
7

Demand Validation (ChennaiOne API)

7,869,058 real trips from ChennaiOne mobility platform (Oct 2025–Mar 2026). Modal split validated. Demand-weighted TFI burden computed. Digital divide assessed.

→ hub_tfi_demand_weighted.csv · 18,389 pax-hrs/day total burden

4.2 The Transfer Friction Index — Formula

Map: Walk Distance Calculation at Hubs
TFI Formula — Transfer Friction Index
TFI (minutes) = [ Walk_Time + Wait_Time + (P_miss × Wait_Time) ] ÷ 60 Walk_Time = gate-to-gate distance (metres) ÷ 1.2 m/s Wait_Time = average inter-departure headway at destination stop (seconds) P_miss = clip( (Walk_Time / 600) × (Wait_Time / 1800), 0.05, 0.40 ) Peak window: 08:00–10:00 and 17:00–19:00 (weekday) Off-Peak window: all other hours with active service Default wait: 900s (15 min) if stop has no GTFS headway data
Worked Example: Pattaravakkam (worst hub, off-peak) Walk distance = 195m → Walk_Time = 195 ÷ 1.2 = 163s = 2.7 min
Off-peak headway = 5,767s = 96 min (bus departs every 96 min)
P_miss = clip((163/600) × (5767/1800), 0.05, 0.40) = clip(0.87, 0.05, 0.40) = 0.40 (capped)
TFI = (163 + 5767 + 0.40 × 5767) ÷ 60 = (163 + 5767 + 2307) ÷ 60 = 8237 ÷ 60 = 137.3 min ✓

6.2 The Double Penalty

Map: Equity Burden Assessment

The most inequitable finding of the demand analysis is what this thesis terms the double penalty: the mode with the highest transfer friction (suburban rail, mean TFI = 32.0 min) also charges the lowest fare (Rs 6.92/trip on average). These are Chennai’s most economically vulnerable transit users — daily wage workers, students from outer suburbs, informal sector employees — who both pay the least and wait the most.

6.4 The Digital Divide — A Critical Caveat

The Burden Is Understated The weekday-to-weekend trip ratio from ChennaiOne is 0.958 — almost identical. A genuine commuter platform would show a strong weekday peak (ratio > 1.3). The near-equal distribution indicates that ChennaiOne users are predominantly discretionary travellers — leisure trips, non-work journeys. The most transit-dependent population — daily wage workers, domestic workers, construction labour — is likely underrepresented in the API data due to the smartphone ownership and digital literacy barriers. The 18,389 pax-hrs/day burden figure is therefore a conservative lower bound.
Chapter 07

Policy Simulation

Three intervention scenarios · The infrastructure equity paradox · Three-phase implementation roadmap.

7.1 Scenario Design

Map: Policy Simulation Scenarios

Three policy scenarios were simulated by modifying the TFI input parameters and recomputing hub scores and the Gini coefficient across all 642 hubs:

  • Scenario A — Timetable Synchronisation: Cap average wait time at 15 minutes (900 seconds) at all hubs where the connecting bus headway exceeds this threshold. Operationally, this represents aligning MTC and Southern Railway departure schedules.
  • Scenario B — Infrastructure (FOB / Walkway): Reduce walk distance by 30% at all hubs (representing construction of foot-over-bridges, covered walkways, and improved pedestrian access). Walk time reduces proportionally.
  • Scenario C — Combined: Both Scenario A and B applied simultaneously.

The Infrastructure Equity Paradox Scenario B (FOB construction, −18.8% mean TFI) actually worsens the Gini coefficient from 0.185 to 0.189 (+2%). Why? Physical walkway improvements reduce walk time — but this primarily benefits central, metro-adjacent hubs where walk distance is the main friction source. Peripheral suburban hubs with 60–96 minute bus headways are NOT helped by shorter walks. Their TFI is dominated by wait time. FOBs without timetable reform are an equity-regressive intervention — they disproportionately benefit already well-served central users while leaving the most burdened peripheral commuters untouched.

7.3 Three-Phase Implementation Roadmap

Phase 1 · 2026–2027 Quick Wins — Schedule Synchronisation
  • Align MTC bus departure times to Southern Railway suburban arrivals at 7 critical hubs
  • Directional signage from station exits to nearest high-frequency bus stops (Urappakkam, Guduvancheri)
  • Introduce dedicated feeder mini-bus service at Pattaravakkam and Veppampattu (6 peak-hour trips)
  • Auto-stand formalisation + covered waiting shelter at VOC Nagar
Lead Agency: MTC + Southern Railway (Chennai Division) Capital Cost: Near-zero (scheduling change) Equity Impact: High — targets worst hubs Saving: ~7,979 pax-hrs/day
Phase 2 · 2028–2031 Structural — Infrastructure & Frequency
  • FOB / covered walkway construction at top-15 betweenness centrality hubs
  • Increase bus frequency on peripheral suburban-bus connector routes (target: max 30-min headway)
  • Real-time digital arrival display boards at all 42 suburban stations in GCC

Chapter 08

Conclusions

Five principal findings, theoretical contributions, policy implications, limitations, and future directions.

8.1 Five Principal Findings

01

Transfer friction is real, measurable, and structurally unequal

Mean TFI = 22.72 min across 642 hubs. Off-peak TFI is 32% higher than peak (+6.24 min average). The worst hub (Pattaravakkam) records 137.8 min off-peak. This is not random variation — it is structural service inequality embedded in scheduling decisions.

02

Spatial inequity is geographically concentrated

Gini = 0.185. Peripheral suburban corridors (northern industrial belt, southern Tambaram–Chengalpattu axis) bear 3× the friction burden of the central metro-served core. Moran’s I confirms spatial autocorrelation — this is systematic, not random.

03

The double penalty disproportionately burdens lowest-income users

Suburban rail: highest mean TFI (32.0 min) + lowest average fare (Rs 6.92). These passengers have the least financial capacity to switch to autos or cabs, yet face the worst structural time penalty. Transit equity and economic equity overlap precisely at this mode.

04

Timetable synchronisation outperforms infrastructure investment for equity

Scenario A (timetable sync): Gini 0.185 → 0.154 (−17%). Scenario B (FOBs): Gini 0.185 → 0.189 (+2% WORSE). Infrastructure-first policy, without timetable reform, is an equity-regressive intervention. The paradox is clear and actionable.

05

Digital demand data understates the burden

ChennaiOne weekday/weekend ratio = 0.958 — indicating discretionary users, not captive workers. The 18,389 pax-hrs/day is a conservative lower bound. Transit-dependent low-income commuters are underrepresented in app data due to digital access barriers.

8.2 Theoretical & Methodological Contributions

First GTFS-derived TFI Framework for an Indian Metro

A composite, reproducible Transfer Friction Index computed from raw GTFS schedule data for all intermodal hubs in a major Indian metropolitan network. Fills a documented gap in Indian urban transport literature.

Temporal Disaggregation of Transfer Quality

Peak vs off-peak TFI split reveals a hidden dimension of transit inequity — the systematic worsening of service quality during off-peak hours that disproportionately affects shift workers and non-standard schedule commuters.

Demand-Weighted Equity Analysis

Integration of real API ridership data with computed friction scores to produce demand-weighted burden metrics — moving beyond theoretical spatial analysis to actual experienced inequality weighted by who uses the network.

Replicable Open-Source Pipeline

Fully documented Python pipeline applicable to any city publishing GTFS feeds. Estimated replication effort: 2–3 weeks for a city with clean GTFS data. Applicable to Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune.

8.3 Policy Implications

MTC — Metropolitan Transport Corporation

Adjust Bus Schedules at 7 Critical Hubs

At VOC Nagar, Veppampattu, Pattaravakkam, Perambur Loco Works, Guduvancheri, Urappakkam, and Maraimalai Nagar Kamarajar: offset MTC bus departures by 8–12 minutes relative to suburban train arrival times. Capital cost: near-zero. Impact: potentially eliminates the worst TFI peaks.

CMRL — Chennai Metro Rail Limited

Embed Transfer Quality in Phase 2 Station Design

Phase 2 station briefs should include a mandatory minimum TFI standard for connecting bus stops (target: <20 min wait) and require gate-to-bus-stop walk distances under 150m. Transfer quality must be a design metric, not an afterthought.

Tamil Nadu Govt / CUMTA

Mandate Inter-Operator Schedule Coordination

CUMTA must be granted binding authority to mandate seasonal schedule alignment between MTC and Southern Railway. A joint schedule coordination committee (MTC + Southern Railway Chennai Division) with quarterly review meetings is the minimum institutional mechanism.

GCC — Greater Chennai Corporation

Incorporate TFI into Development Planning

TFI scores should be incorporated into ward-level development indicators. High-TFI peripheral wards should receive priority in urban local body transit improvement budgets. Real-time TFI monitoring using GTFS-realtime data should be included in the GCC Smart City dashboard.

8.4 Limitations

  1. GTFS Static Data: No real-time delay data incorporated. Actual TFI during disruptions will be higher than computed values.
  2. Weekday Only: Weekend service variability not measured. Weekend off-peak may be significantly worse.
  3. ChennaiOne Coverage: API captures approximately 10–15% of actual ridership. Captive workers severely underrepresented.
  4. Walk Time Model: Assumes 1.2 m/s flat terrain. Heat, grade, barriers, and pedestrian safety not modelled.
  5. No Stated Preference Survey: Transfer penalty valuation uses literature-derived assumptions (2× IVT) rather than Chennai-specific stated preference data.
  6. Phase 2 CMRL GTFS Unavailable: Future Phase 2 stations not modelled. Post-2028 network will have significantly different TFI distribution.

8.5 Future Research Directions

  1. GTFS-realtime integration for dynamic, real-time TFI monitoring and passenger alerts.
  2. Extension to the full Chennai Metropolitan Area (CMA) when Southern Railway GTFS coverage improves.
  3. Stated preference survey (n=500+) for Chennai-specific transfer penalty valuation parameters.
  4. Gender-disaggregated safety analysis at transfer hubs — night-time safety perceptions at low-TFI hubs.
  5. Machine learning models for predictive TFI based on weather, events, and seasonal patterns.
  6. Replication study for Mumbai, Delhi, and Bangalore to produce comparative all-India TFI benchmarks.
“Chennai’s transit challenge is not primarily one of missing infrastructure — it is one of missing coordination. The Transfer Friction Index demonstrates that schedule misalignment between independently operated agencies creates a measurable, spatially inequitable burden that falls disproportionately on the city’s most economically vulnerable commuters. The remedy is political and administrative as much as it is technical.”

A.3 Worked TFI Example — Pattaravakkam Off-Peak

Step-by-Step TFI Calculation
Station: PATTARAVAKKAM (suburban) Transfer to: AMBATTUR GNANAMOORTHY NAGAR (bus) Step 1 — Walk Distance: Gate-to-gate distance = 370 metres Walk speed = 1.2 m/s (HCM standard) Walk_Time = 370 ÷ 1.2 = 308s = 5.1 min Step 2 — Wait Time (Off-Peak): Bus departures at this stop: 9 trips between 06:00–22:00 Average headway = (22×60 - 6×60) min ÷ 9 trips ≈ 96 min = 5,760s Step 3 — Missed Connection Probability: P_miss = clip( (308/600) × (5760/1800), 0.05, 0.40 ) = clip( 0.513 × 3.20, 0.05, 0.40 ) = clip( 1.64, 0.05, 0.40 ) = 0.40 [capped at 40% maximum] Step 4 — TFI: TFI = (308 + 5760 + 0.40 × 5760) ÷ 60 = (308 + 5760 + 2304) ÷ 60 = 8372 ÷ 60 = 139.5 minutes off-peak Interpretation: A passenger arriving at Pattaravakkam suburban station during off-peak hours faces an expected 139.5 minutes of total transfer friction — walk penalty + wait penalty + missed connection penalty.
Jury FAQ

Anticipated Questions & Answers

Prepared responses to the most likely questions from the evaluation panel.

1. Why is TFI so high at some hubs — is this actually happening?
Yes — the TFI values are directly derived from GTFS departure records. At VOC Nagar, the connecting MTC bus (Tondiarpet-2 Depot) has exactly 16 departure records spaced at 60-minute intervals (05:39, 06:39, 07:39…). A passenger who misses that bus waits up to 60 minutes. The reason most people don’t personally experience this is because informed commuters use apps to time their arrival, or take autos as alternatives. That behavioural adaptation IS the burden — extra cognitive effort, financial cost (auto fare), or lost time. The TFI captures the structural service supply gap, not what a schedule-aware passenger manages to navigate around.
2. Why use the Gini coefficient for transit equity?
The Gini is a standard, internationally comparable measure of distributional inequality. Applied to TFI: Gini = 0 means all 642 hubs have identical TFI (perfect equality); Gini = 1 means all friction is concentrated at one hub. Chennai’s 0.185 means the top-20% most-burdened wards absorb 47% of total friction — moderate but spatially concentrated inequality. Prior work (Jang et al. 2017, Welch & Mishra 2013) uses identical methodology, providing methodological precedent and comparability.
3. How do you validate TFI without primary field surveys?
Three-tier validation: (1) GTFS consistency check — departure times verified internally consistent; (2) ChennaiOne API — 7.87M real trips confirm modal splits align with GTFS-implied service levels; (3) Field survey at 4 nodes (Chennai Central, Guindy, St. Thomas Mount, Alandur; n=50 each) — Pearson correlation between computed TFI and reported wait time was r=1.000 at all 4 nodes (small sample, indicative). The GTFS is ground truth for scheduled service — the limitation is it does not capture real-time delays.
4. Isn’t the TFI formula subjective?
Walk_Time and Wait_Time are entirely objective (directly from GTFS data). The only assumed component is P_miss — a clip function bounded 0.05–0.40. Sensitivity analysis: varying these bounds ±50% changes mean TFI by only ±3.2% and Gini by ±0.004. The formula is robust. The functional form has logical foundation: longer walks and longer headways both increase the probability of missing a connection due to delays.
5. Why does Scenario B (infrastructure) worsen equity?
The Infrastructure Equity Paradox: FOBs reduce walk distance, but the dominant friction at peripheral high-TFI hubs is wait time (60–96 minute bus headways), not walk time. Reducing walk distance by 30% at Pattaravakkam saves ~1.5 min walk but does nothing about the 96-minute wait. Central metro-adjacent hubs benefit substantially from FOBs because walk IS their friction source. So infrastructure flows to the already well-served, while the worst-burdened remain untouched. Gini worsens as a result.
6. What does a Gini of 0.185 mean in context?
Transit Gini values range from ~0.08 (Tokyo) to ~0.38 (São Paulo). Chennai’s 0.185 is moderate — not extreme, but actionable. The key is the spatial pattern: Moran’s I confirms high-TFI hubs cluster geographically in specific zones. This means the inequity is structural and addressable through targeted intervention — not random variation requiring network-wide investment.
7. Why only GCC, not the full CMA?
GTFS data quality is the binding constraint. Beyond the GCC boundary, Southern Railway GTFS coverage for the Chengalpattu–Arakkonam–Tiruvallur fringe is incomplete — many stations have missing stop_times records. Including CMA would introduce systematic underestimation of TFI in outer zones, making spatial equity comparisons misleading. GCC provides near-complete trimodal GTFS coverage for rigorous, internally consistent computation across all 200 wards.
8. What is your single most important policy recommendation?
Mandate timetable coordination between MTC and Southern Railway at the 7 critical suburban transfer hubs as a Phase 1 intervention. Specifically: offset MTC bus departure times at Tondiarpet-2 Depot (VOC Nagar), Veppampet (Veppampattu), and Ambattur Gnanamoorthy Nagar (Pattaravakkam) by 8–12 minutes relative to scheduled suburban train arrivals. Capital cost: near-zero. Estimated impact: ~7,979 passenger-hours saved per day, Gini improves 17%.
9. How does Chennai compare to other Indian cities?
No comparable GTFS-based TFI study exists for other Indian cities — this is itself a contribution. Qualitative context: Mumbai suburban rail has near-zero wait TFI (2–5 min headways) but extreme walk distances at crowded terminals. Delhi DMRC+DTC has institutional integration (Unified Ticketing) but no published TFI analysis. Chennai’s unique challenge is Southern Railway involvement — a Central Government entity with separate scheduling authority, creating a more complex coordination problem than purely state-operated networks.
10. Can this pipeline be applied to other cities?
Yes — the pipeline requires only GTFS data and ward/boundary shapefiles. GTFS is available (partially or fully) for Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune, Ahmedabad, and Kochi. The Python codebase is modular — each of the seven pipeline stages runs independently. Estimated replication: 2–3 weeks for data preparation + 1 week for analysis, for a city with clean GTFS data.
