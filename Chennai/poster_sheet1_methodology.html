Measuring Transfer Friction and Temporal Inequity
in Chennai's Multimodal Public Transport Network

Research Framework & Methodology — A schedule-based operational analysis of CMRL Metro, MTC Bus & Southern Railway using GTFS data to develop a Transfer Friction Index and evaluate temporal inequity across interchange nodes in Chennai.

01Research Context
1.7M Stop-time records processed
from GTFS static feeds
3 Transit modes integrated:
CMRL · MTC Bus · Southern Railway
~8,000 Walkable intermodal transfer
edges ≤ 500 m gate-to-gate
Research Problem: Urban transit planning in Chennai optimises spatial connectivity while the temporal experience of passengers at intermodal transfer nodes remains unmeasured. Peak-hour schedules are uncoordinated across CMRL, MTC, and Southern Railway — creating hidden time penalties concentrated in peripheral zones.
02Research Gap & Objectives
1
No unified TFI metric exists for Indian cities that integrates walk time, wait time, and missed connection probability into a single scalar index for multimodal networks.
2
Spatial inequity unmeasured: Peripheral vs. central core transfer burdens have never been quantified using Gini-based network analysis for Chennai.
3
Centroid-based walk estimates: Existing studies use station centroid distances. Precise entry/exit gate polygon data has not been incorporated in any Indian study.
4
No policy simulation: Schedule synchronisation vs. physical infrastructure (FOBs) improvements have never been compared computationally for Indian multimodal contexts.

05Transfer Friction Index — Core Metric
The Transfer Friction Index (TFI) — Novel Composite Metric
TFI = Wt + Ww + ( Pmiss × Ww )
Wt
Walk Time
gate-to-gate, seconds
Ww
Average Wait Time
headway-based proxy
Pmiss
Missed Connection Prob.
range [0.05 – 0.40]

Probability formulation: Pmiss = clip [ (Walkt / 600) × (Waitw / 1800), 0.05, 0.40 ] — scales with walk distance and service headway. High-frequency metro nodes receive low Pmiss; peripheral suburban stops with long headways receive high penalties.

06Six-Stage Analytical Pipeline
1
Data Ingestion
Standardise GTFS feeds; filter 1.7M stop-times to Weekday; parse calendar.txt across all three agencies
2
Network Builder
Snap geometries to gate polygons; construct ~8,000 walk transfer edges at gate-to-gate impedance ≤ 500 m
3
Graph Analysis
Build directed topological graph; compute Betweenness Centrality to identify critical backbone hubs
4
TFI Computation
Segment Peak/Off-Peak; calculate per-node headways; apply TFI formula across all intermodal edges
5
Inequity Analysis
Compute Gini Coefficient of TFI; spatial hotspot clustering; zone-level burden classification
6
Policy Simulation
Simulate Scenario A: Timetable Sync; B: FOB Infrastructure; C: Combined — quantify TFI reduction
07Temporal Analysis Windows
Peak Hours
08:00–10:00
17:00–19:00

Morning and evening commute windows. Highest demand. Peripheral nodes show extreme friction spikes — up to 90+ minutes per transfer despite high ridership pressure.

Off-Peak Hours
10:00–17:00
19:00–08:00

Midday and night windows. Suburban rail off-peak headways of 45–90 min create severe schedule instability. TFI at some nodes exceeds peak values during late-night hours.

08Key Methodological Innovations
A

Gate-to-Gate Accuracy

Entry/exit polygon geometries replace centroid distances — essential for accurate physical walk impedance at metro and suburban rail stations where platforms are set back from centroids.

B

Probabilistic Missed Connection

Unlike static studies, TFI includes a probabilistic term that penalises low-frequency links — capturing real passenger risk of missing connections at suburban transfer nodes.

C

Peak / Off-Peak Segmentation

Temporal disaggregation of 1.7M records reveals how TFI distribution shifts dramatically between periods — invisible in aggregate analyses of multimodal accessibility.

D

Gini-Based Equity Metric

Adapts welfare economics Gini Coefficient to measure spatial inequality of transfer burden — operationalises equity as a measurable, comparable urban planning indicator.
