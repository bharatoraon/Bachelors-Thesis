Chennai is one of India's Principal Urban Agglomerations with a rapidly expanding multimodal transit network. Despite substantial investment in metro rail infrastructure, transport planning has historically focused on spatial network expansion and route coverage, while the temporal experience of intermodal transfers — the weak link in the multimodal chain — remains unmeasured and unaddressed. Schedule misalignment between the three independently operated agencies creates hidden time penalties concentrated in peripheral zones and among transit-dependent communities.
02Problem Statement

The Core Problem: Hidden Temporal Inequity at Intermodal Transfer Nodes

  • 1
    Urban public transport planning in Indian metropolitan contexts predominantly emphasises physical network expansion, route coverage, and infrastructure provision — while ignoring the temporal experience of passengers during intermodal transfers.
  • 2
    In multimodal systems, delays arising from poorly coordinated schedules, extended waiting times, and unreliable transfers can substantially inflate total travel time, producing unequal accessibility outcomes across the city's spatial hierarchy.
  • 3
    These temporal inefficiencies remain invisible within conventional distance-based planning evaluations — spatial accessibility metrics substantially overestimate effective access when temporal reliability and schedule coordination are excluded.
  • 4
    Peripheral and suburban areas with physically adequate transit infrastructure experience disproportionately high transfer friction — a burden that is both structurally unmeasured and systematically unaddressed in current Chennai transport planning practice.
  • 5
    Transfer inefficiencies are demonstrably primarily operational rather than infrastructural — indicating that significant improvement is achievable through schedule coordination alone, without requiring large-scale physical construction programmes.
03Literature Review & Research Gap
Levine & Grengs (2010) · Pereira et al. (2017)

Time-Based Accessibility

Schedule reliability fundamentally shapes reachable opportunities. Advocates replacing proximity-based metrics with travel-time thresholds that include transfer and waiting costs.

Guo & Wilson (2011) · Iseki & Taylor (2009)

Transfer Penalty Studies

Quantify transfer penalties using level-of-service metrics. Passengers penalise waiting time 2–3× more than in-vehicle time in modal choice models. Walk must be measured gate-to-gate.

Rieser et al. (2010) · Dibbelt et al. (2015)

Multimodal Network Modelling

Time-expanded network representations of GTFS-based public transport. Establish methodological basis for schedule-sensitive multimodal network analysis at scale.

Pereira et al. (2019) · Grengs (2012)

Transport Equity & Gini

Gini-based metrics applied to measure distributional fairness of transit access. Demonstrate systematic disadvantage for peripheral zones and lower-income commuter populations.

RITES Studies · Urban India Journal

Indian Urban Transit

Existing Indian studies focus on route coverage and bus frequency. No prior study develops a composite TFI for Indian multimodal networks using GTFS with gate-polygon precision.

Wessel et al. (2017) · Bast et al. (2010)

GTFS Data Applications

GTFS as computational substrate for accessibility analysis. Schedule filtering, headway derivation, and time-expanded trip planning algorithms established in international literature.

04Aim, Objectives, Scope & Limitations
Research Aim

To measure, map, and explain the extent and spatial distribution of transfer friction and temporal inequity within Chennai's multimodal public transport network — and to evaluate policy interventions capable of reducing this inequity through operationally feasible planning mechanisms.

O1

Develop the Transfer Friction Index

Formulate a transferable, computation-ready TFI for multimodal networks using GTFS and GIS data.

O2

Construct Time-Expanded Network

Build a multimodal network for Chennai incorporating exact gate-to-gate walk impedance.

O3

Evaluate Temporal Variability

Assess spatial and temporal variability of TFI across Peak and Off-Peak service periods.

O4

Ward-Level Temporal Accessibility

Assess ward-level accessibility and link findings to demographic vulnerability indicators.

O5

Simulate Policy Scenarios

Compare timetable synchronisation vs. physical FOB infrastructure in terms of TFI reduction.

O6

Planning Recommendations

Formulate governance proposals for embedding temporal equity metrics in statutory frameworks.

Scope

  • Weekday schedule operations only (GTFS static data)
  • Three modes: CMRL Metro, MTC Bus, Southern Railway
  • Transfer threshold: walk distance ≤ 500 m (gate-to-gate)
  • Temporal windows: Peak (08–10, 17–19) and Off-Peak
  • Spatial extent: Greater Chennai Corporation wards
  • Policy simulation via computational scenario modelling
  • Methodology transferable to other Indian metros

Limitations

  • GTFS static data — no real-time delay or disruption data
  • Weekend and holiday schedules excluded from analysis
  • Passenger volume / boarding data not incorporated
  • Auto-rickshaw and informal transit modes not modelled
  • Census 2011 demographics — pre-pandemic data baseline
  • Walk speed assumed uniform 1.2 m/s (no terrain gradient)
  • Gate polygon data may have minor positional variation
Significance: This study reframes multimodal evaluation around passenger time experience, introducing the necessity for temporal performance metrics in urban transport planning — applicable across all Indian multimodal transit contexts.

09Identification of Key Issues & Potential Interventions

Issue 1: Schedule Fragmentation

CMRL, MTC, and Southern Railway operate on fully independent timetables with no inter-agency coordination mechanism. Connecting service departures are not synchronised, creating wait times of 30–90 min at peripheral suburban nodes during off-peak hours.

Potential: Cross-agency timetable integration authority; mandate ≤15 min max connecting interval at all major interchange nodes. Computer-aided schedule optimisation using TFI outputs as the primary performance KPI.

Issue 2: Peripheral Frequency Gap

Southern Railway suburban services at peripheral nodes (Veppampattu, VOC Nagar, Pattaravakkam) operate at headways of 45–90 minutes during off-peak — 9–15× longer than central metro intervals. Transit-dependent outer-city commuters bear structurally inequitable burdens.

Potential: Targeted frequency augmentation on high-friction Southern Railway corridors to ≤20 min off-peak. Dedicated shuttle-bus last-mile services at suburban stations to fill temporal gaps.

Issue 3: Walk Infrastructure Deficit

Gate-to-gate analysis reveals walk distances of 250–350 m at major bus-rail interchanges. Without grade-separated crossings or covered walkways, the physical walk time is amplified — adding friction beyond the schedule-derived component.

Potential: Prioritised FOB/subway construction at nodes where walk distance exceeds 250 m and TFI >50 min. Integrated wayfinding and signage between modes to reduce cognitive and physical walk friction.

Issue 4: Measurement & Governance Gap

Chennai's transport agencies lack a standardised, shared performance metric for intermodal transfer quality. Without measurable KPIs, temporal inequity is structurally invisible in planning reports, budget allocations, and policy reviews.

Potential: Statutory adoption of TFI as a mandatory performance indicator in Chennai MaaS platform and CMDA master plan reviews. Real-time digital dashboard for continuous monitoring and annual equity reporting.

Potential 1: MaaS Integration

The TFI framework — built entirely on open GTFS data — is directly embeddable in Mobility-as-a-Service platforms. Real-time TFI scoring can integrate with trip planners (Google Maps, ONDC) to surface transfer-optimised routes for passengers.

Opportunity: API-ready TFI computation engine for MaaS integration. Passenger-facing Transfer Quality Score displayed at trip booking stage for informed modal choice.

Potential 2: Transferable Methodology

The six-stage GTFS-to-TFI pipeline is methodologically generalisable. Any Indian city with GTFS data (Mumbai, Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune) can apply this framework without city-specific customisation, contributing to a national equity monitoring infrastructure.

Opportunity: Open-source TFI toolkit for adoption by urban transport planning authorities across India. First transferable multimodal transfer equity measurement framework for Indian metros.
